Building an ETL data pipeline 🪂

To be able to transform data into powerful insights, it is necessary to set up a proper Extract, Transform and Load (ETL) pipeline. By linking data from different sources and transforming it according to rules relevant for your business, you can build a solid fundament for your reporting, business intelligence or statistical/machine learning modelling.

Building a data pipeline may consist of:

  • Collecting data from structured sources (e.g. databases, cloud storage solutions, survey tools, software that generates data or other curated datasets)
  • Collecting data from unstructured sources (e.g. web scraping)
  • Data clean-up (e.g. restructuring the data, repairing logical errors, etc.)
  • Applying business logic to the data to make it truly meaningful
  • Combining data from different sources and/or building a data model
  • Publishing the output data to a final storage solution so they can be reused across projects (e.g. a database, cloud storage, network drive, etc.)

Designing interactive BI reports 📊

The way to unlocking the potential of your data often starts with getting to know your data and what better way to understand your data than to visualise it in a way that's meaningful to your organisation? Whether you want to track real-time metrics or follow important developments in quarterly or annual KPIs, identify difference between different categories of products, customers or employees, an interactive, automatically updated report can facilitate you in achieving your goals.

Designing a business intelligence (BI) solution may include:

  • Building an ETL data pipeline and/or a data model to facilitate data import
  • Performing additional calculations to support data visualisation
  • Creating different data visualisations and adding different functionalities to the report (e.g. the ability to use filters and slicers on the data or change the metrics shown on the charts)
  • Designing and testing the user interface (UI) of the report in collaboration with end users
  • Documenting any calculations and assumptions made in the model
  • Onboarding end users to ensure they can make the best possible use of the new product

Running statistical analysis and hypothesis testing ⚖️

While data visualisation is a great way to start exploring the potential of your data, a chart is seldom sufficient to tell you the story behind what you see on it. To dig deeper and ensure that the insights you're getting have a solid backing, it is often useful to do some sort of hypothesis testing or study the impact of different factors on the metric you're interested in explaining. With the right kind of statistical analysis, you will not only be able to discern between apparent and real patterns but also measure important differences and effects observed in the data.

Running a statistical analysis may include:

  • Data collection and preparation for use in the analysis
  • Defining and testing hypotheses
  • Explaining correlations and interrelationships in your data
  • Identifying outlier observations in your data
  • Measuring the impact of certain factors on a given outcome (e.g. how many additional sales can be generated by giving a discount or what the likelihood that a customer will cancel their subscription is based on their recent behaviour)
  • Summing up on the conclusions and communicating them to the relevant stakeholders

Building predictive statistical/ML models 🔮

Data-mature organisations use a mixture of approaches to extract the most value from their data and stay competitive. With the recent explosion in generative AI, the edge that applying methods more sophisticated than traditional statistical analysis has been getting the spotlight. In reality, both machine learning (ML) and traditional statistics can be useful when it comes to building predictive models. The choice of whether to use one thing or the other will often depend on your circumstances, such as the quality and quantity of the data you have available. Either way, having a predictive model at your disposal can be a powerful tool to supercharge your decision-making.

Building predictive models may include:

  • Defining the model's input and output variables
  • Data collection and preparation for use in the analysis
  • Training, testing and optimising different kinds of algorithms to improve model accuracy
  • Using the optimised model to generate predictions for the outcome variable
  • Setting up a recurrent model training and predictions generation

Onboarding and training of non-technical people 🧑‍🏫

If you find yourself in a situation where either you or someone else in your company feels unable to use the outputs of your analytics/data science team, then you may benefit from a proper introduction to those tools and the concepts they build upon.

Evaluating and optimising the use of your data-driven insights 🙋‍♀️

You may have an excellent data set-up at your organisation and use the most cutting-edge technologies to derive insights but if people are not able to use these tools or understand how you've arrived at your conclusion, you may not be using your data to your full potential. Explainability is a particular challenge for machine learning (ML) models but can also extend to interactive reporting tools or even statistical analysis, where data and results may easily be misinterpreted.

With the right kind of communication and user onboarding, you can minimise the risk that your work may end up unused or, perhaps even worse, used in the wrong way, leading to making harmful decisions for your organisation, its customers and employees.

Introducing leaders to technical terms and concepts 🧑‍💻

Alternatively, you may find yourself in charge of a highly technical team even though you may not necessarily have the same background as them. Your excellent leadership skills will go a long way in ensuring the team has the right inputs to accomplish their goals, but you may feel a bit uneasy about all the unfamiliar concepts they casually throw around you. If you feel like you'd like to learn more about the tools your team is using but are limited on time or fear falling down the trap of getting bogged down by all the details, you may benefit from a proper introduction to the tools and concepts used in modern data management.

Whether you'd like to know more about the basics of programming using R or Python, dive deeper into the obvious and more obscure functionalities offered by Excel or Power BI, MindGraph can support you with getting the right amount of technical knowledge needed to better understand and support your team.
